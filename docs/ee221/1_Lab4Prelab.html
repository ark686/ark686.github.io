Lab 4 Prelab: MOSFET Amplifier ​

1. Overview ​

In this lab, students design and implement a single-stage MOSFET amplifier and explore the frequency response of the amplifier. The students will use LTspice to simulate the amplifier and then use the Analog Discovery 2 data acquisition module and WaveForms software to experimentally plot the amplifier response.

2. Health and Safety ​

Any laboratory environment may contain conditions that are potentially hazardous to a person's health if not handled appropriately. The Electrical & Computer Engineering laboratories obviously have electrical potentials that may be lethal and must be treated with respect. In addition, there are also mechanical hazards, particularly when dealing with rotating machines, and chemical hazards because of the materials used in various components. One learning outcome is to educate all laboratory users to be able to handle laboratory materials and situations safely and thereby ensure a safe and healthy experience for all. Watch for posted information in and around the laboratories, and on the class web site.

4. Background ​

4.1. MOSFET Amplifier Design ​

Consider the MOSFET N-Channel amplifier circuit shown in Figure 1. It is meant to amplify input signal "Vin" to output signal "Vout".

Figure 1: MOSFET Amplifier Circuit Schematic

The following analysis is appropriate for good quality transistors where the output current, IDS, is largely independent of the voltage VDS (the output characteristic curves are approximately "flat"). The small signal FET equivalent circuit is shown in Figure 2. We can calculate the amplifier AC gain using the small signal FET transconductance "gm" and we assume "ro" can be neglected because it is large in comparison to other circuit resistances, therefore gain = —gm × Rd. The input impedance is essentially (R1 || R2) and the output impedance is equal to Rd if ro is large. The small signal AC gain is calculated assuming that the capacitors have negligible impedance.

Figure 2: Small Signal FET Equivalent Circuit

The amplifier gain can be found by:

vin=vgs+idRs=idgm+idRsvout=−idRdAv=voutvin=−idRdidgm+idRs=−Rd1gm+Rs=−gmRd1+gmRs

Transconductance gm can be calculated by:

gm=2×IDSVOVVOV=VGS−Vth

where VGS is the gate-to-source voltage required to achieve a target IDS and Vth is the threshold (turn-on) voltage for the transistor.

Rd can be determined from VRd and ID. We want VoutDC=0 to allow maximum output voltage swing for Vout, and thus:

Rd=VRdID

Given a value for Av, and calculated gm and Rd, the value for Rs can be calculated.

The voltage at the MOSFET gate, VG (relative to signal ground), is set by the values of resistors R1 and R2 and must be equal to:

VG=VGS+IDS×Rs−(5V)

Determine R1 and R2 which achieve the required VG voltage while also having a required input impedance > MIN_INPUT_IMPEDANCE. This means that (R1 || R2) > MIN_INPUT_IMPEDANCE and thus both R1 and R2 must be greater than (2 X MIN_INPUT_IMPEDANCE). Select an appropriate value for R2 and then calculate R1 using:

VG=R2R1+R2×(10V)−(5V)

5. Simulation ​

In this section, design and simulate a MOSFET amplifier using a 2N7000 N-Channel MOSFET transistor. Use LTspice to perform the circuit simulations outlined in this section.

5.1. 2N7000 N-Channel MOSFET IDS-VGS Characteristics ​

Consider the circuit shown in Figure 3. Determine the Gate-Source voltage (V_GS) which results in an IDS of 20 mA.

Figure 3: V_GS for I_DS Circuit

Simulate the circuit shown Figure 3 in LTspice. Set V1 to 5 V and V_GS ranging from 0 V to 5 V in 0.01 V steps (e.g. ".dc V2 0 5 0.01").

Use the following "model" for the 2N7000 MOSFET:

text
.MODEL 2N7000 NMOS (LEVEL=1 VTO=1.7 KP=.17 GAMMA=1.76U
+ PHI=.75 LAMBDA=5M RD=.35 RS=.448 IS=41.6F PB=.8 MJ=.46
+ CBD=44.4P CBS=53.3P CGSO=24N CGDO=20N CGBO=116N)

Plot V_DS versus V_GS. Your results should look similar to Figure 4 (Note: This plot was made for a different transistor, so may not be exactly the same as your results).

When IDS=20 mA (which is the target current), the voltage VDS=(5 V)−IDS×(100 Ω)=3 V. Attach a "cursor" to VGS and determine the VGS for VDS = 3 V. Use this value for VGS in the design of the amplifier (Cursor 1 in Figure 4).

Also determine VGS when VDS just starts to change from 5 V. Use this value for Vth in the design of the amplifier (Cursor 2 in Figure 4).

Include a screen shot of your LTspice circuit and plot in your lab report.

Figure 4: Simulated VDS versus VGS

5.2. Design the Amplifier ​

Design an amplifier of the form shown in Figure 1 with the following characteristics using the equations from Section 4.1:

  • 2N7000 N-Channel MOSFET Transistor
  • Gain = −2 (negative indicates inverting)
  • IDS=20 mA
  • VDD=+5 V
  • VSS=−5 V
  • Minimum input impedance = 10 kΩ (R1 and R2 must each be > 10 kΩ)
  • VoutDC = 0 V

Determine:

  • Rd, Rs, R1 and R2

Show your calculations in your lab report.

Answer the following questions in your lab report:

  • What are the impedances of C1 and C2 at 1 kHz?
  • What is the purpose of capacitors C1 and C2?

5.3. Simulate the Amplifier ​

Construct the designed amplifier circuit in LTspice including a 100 kΩ load resistor on the output. Your LTspice circuit should look similar to Figure 5. Note that the resistor values are not filled in; fill in with the values you determined in the previous section. Set Vin to a 200 mV 1 kHz sine signal.

Figure 5: LTspice Amplifier Circuit

Perform a "Transient" simulation for 4 ms (".tran 4m") and plot "Vin" and "Vout". The results should look similar to Figure 6 (indicating the output is twice the amplitude of the input but shifted by 180°).

Your output voltage MUST be within ±10% of the target voltage (i.e., in the range 720 - 880 mV peak-to-peak) for your lab to be accepted.

Include a screen shot of your LTspice circuit and plot in your lab report.

Figure 6: LTspice Amplifier Vin/Vout Simulation

Use LTspice to perform a "DC operating point" simulation (".op"). This should generate a window similar to Figure 7, verifying that I(Rd) is close to 20 mA and V(voutdc) is close to 0 V. Include a screen shot of this window in your lab report.

Your simulated I(Rd) MUST be within 10% of the target current (i.e., in the range 18 - 22 mA) AND your simulated V(voutdc) must be in the range ±100 mV for your lab to be accepted.
